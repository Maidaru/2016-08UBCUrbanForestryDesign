What's eco-HOME?

       Located south of Wesbrook neighborhood, eco-HOME Neighborhood connects Pacific Spirit Regional Park, UBC south campass and Wesbrook Neighborhood, providing a convinence living hood with strong bonds between human and nature with a Learning Centre that aims to educate the public on the relationship between people and nature. As our name inteprets,

  • H for health and well-being
  • O for open spaces and orecreation
  • M for mutual respect
  • E for ecology and education
    • Our Vision

           In modern society today, ecology awareness gradually plays a more and more important role around the world. We design the community to be a perfectly green and eco-friendly one. We would like to make our residence fit into the nature, instead of making a totally human-direction one. We want to make Eco-Home a sustainable society that People could enjoy the ecosystem service nearby, such as Pacific Spirit Regional Park, and, at the same time, experience the significance of the ecology. Our design aims to respect and protect nature, make people become a part of nature and ensure next generation could feel the throb and beauty of nature in the future.

           To reach these goals, we have a few expectation about the community we would be happy to see in the future. Ecology is the point we emphasis on. We expect that people could become a part of nature. The place where residents relax and exercise is just the wildlife habitat and green corridor. People will feel easy to have wildlife and trees in their daily life. Moreover, we want to merge the Pacific Spirit Regional Park into the community culture. The central park could connect all the residents and the learning center could become a green study space not only for residents but for UBC faculties.

    In our community, we have Ginkgo (Ginkgo biloba var. aurea), Black Locust (Robinia pseudoacacia), Red Maple (Acer rubrum), Northern Red Oak (Quercus rubra), Euro-pean Beech (Fagus sylvatica), and London Plane (Platanus acerifolia). All of them are common in UBC campus.

    We use two kinds of evergreen species, two golden-leaf species, and two kinds of color-changing species. In fall, you can see at least three different colors in our community!

    European Beech

    Scientific name: Fagus sylvatica

    Trunk diameter can be 3 meters. Generally, it is 25-35 meters height, and diameter is 1.5 meters.

    In the good illumination, individual growing tree has shorter stem, bigger crown, wider covering range, and long panches.

    Location: Surrounding the community.

    American Elm

    Scientific name: Ulmus pumila L.

    American Elm can grow 40 meters height, and more than one meters diameter. The shape of its branches is a smooth arc, so if you plant American Elm along the both sides of trail, it will form like a tunnel, providing a complete shade.

    Location: Jogging trail.

    Ginkgo

    Scientific name: Ginkgo biloba var. aurea

    This kind of Ginkgo is the variant of the Ginkgo we see generally. Ginkgo biloba is the oldest extant seed plants relict plant, known as living fossils. Its seed is famous as Chinese herbal medicine.

    Location: The Playground and between Building B and C.

    Black Locust

    Scientific name: Robinia pseudoacacia

    Black Locust has well-adaptation, it likes illumination, but afraid of too much water. In spring, the color of leaves is golden, then turn to be yellow-green in summer, and orange in fall. It blossoms at the beginning of summer, and its white flower has fragrance.

    Location: The Stage Square.

    Northern Red Oak

    Specific name: Quercus rubra

    Northern Red Oak can grow 27 meters height, and the crown width is 15 meters on average. It can tolerate drought, low temperature, barren, fire, shade,and pollution. It likes illumination, and has well-adaptation.

    Location: The Entrance to Pacific Spirit Regional park and in front of Ecological Pond.

    Red Maple

    Scientific name: Acer rubrum

    Red maple can grow 18-27 meters height, diameter at breast height is about 60 centimeters, and the shape of leaf is 3-5 palmate lobes with a serrated margin. The back of the leaves is gray-white. It blossoms in spring, the flower is red, its samara is red as well.

    Location: The Playground and between Building B and C.

    Figure 1: Storm water management plan

    The runoffs from the green roofs are collected via the storm water pipes. They are pretreated to remove and solids and stored underground. The pump channels the water for irrigation and the overflow is drained into the ground. The pre treated water is also pumped to the storm water pond and the excess is stored in an underground aquifer. The aquifer pumps back the water to the pond during the dry months. In case of extreme flooding, the excess water will be channeled into a dry detention pond.

    Here is our learning centre which is the major spot we connect with forest. Around with various kinds of plants, the building looks more ecological. There exists a trail through the centre which is a key to open the forest.

    At the first ground, most exquisite souvenirs attract people’s eyes, you can buy some flower seeds and books about Canadian trees. Most importantly, a large exhibition room stands at the centre at the first floor. We can see the overall layout of the forest and community clearly.

    Walk upstairs, you can see tons of books neatly on the shelf. They are all the books telling us trees’story. Then on the third floor, it is a good place for kids to recognize and study the nature. There is the other approach that citizens can easily enter the forest.

    Locating near the learning centre, a big tree with board trail show us the natural beauty, people can enjoy the forest trip followed by tour guide as well as explore the forest by themselves. In addition, on the northern side, a jogging trail between two groves lead us to the forest. Individuals can breath clean air and enjoy running entertainment which are also benefit to their health. Therefore, on the basis of environmental protection, we strengthen interaction between people and nature. Encouraging them to be involved in forest activities is a good idea to enhance the bond.
